A brief on unreasonable hospitality

The last 5%
is where the
magic lives.

Unreasonable Hospitality is a book, a philosophy, and a way of working. It's about what separates good from remarkable, and why the difference almost always lives in a decision someone chose to make. At Big Spaceship, we make those decisions every day — in how we show up for our clients, how we treat each other, and how much of ourselves we're willing to put into the work. This is about doing all of that with intention.

The premise

A restaurant.
A philosophy.
A brief.

Will Guidara co-ran Eleven Madison Park — a restaurant in New York that became #1 in the world. But his book isn't really about restaurants. It's about what happens when you decide, consciously and repeatedly, to treat the people in front of you as if they are the most important people in the room.

The insight isn't complicated: most organizations get good at the fundamentals and stop there. They deliver. They execute. They hit the brief. Guidara's argument is that excellence at the fundamentals isn't the destination — it's the permission structure. It earns you the right to do something extraordinary. And extraordinary is always a choice.

Four principles

What it actually
means.

01
Make it
unreasonable

The word "unreasonable" is the whole point. Reasonable hospitality is a hotel that checks you in on time. Unreasonable hospitality is a hotel that noticed you mentioned your anniversary in passing and left champagne in the room. No one asked them to. No one would have complained if they hadn't. They chose to.

Guidara's team had a line they used internally: "What's the most unreasonable thing we could do right now?" Not irresponsible. Not excessive. Unreasonable — meaning it goes further than anyone would have expected, further than was required, further than was safe.

The unreasonable gesture is always the one people remember. It's the one they tell someone about. It's the one that turns a transaction into a relationship.

At Big Spaceship

When was the last time we delivered something that made a client say "you didn't have to do that"? That's the zone. Not in scope creep — in the small, considered gesture that signals we're paying attention to them as people, not just a project.

02
Read
the room

Unreasonable hospitality can't be scripted. A checklist of nice gestures isn't the same thing — it's just a more elaborate standard. What Guidara describes is something closer to radical attentiveness: being present enough to notice what someone actually needs, not just what they asked for.

His teams were trained to look for signals. A couple eating quietly might be celebrating something, or grieving something — and the right response to each is completely different. The job wasn't to run the protocol. The job was to read what was actually happening and respond to that.

This requires slowing down enough to see. In a culture that optimizes for speed and throughput, presence is a competitive advantage.

At Big Spaceship

What are the signals our clients are giving us that we're too busy to catch? Stress in a feedback call. A stakeholder who's gone quiet. A brief that says one thing but means another. Reading the room is a creative skill.

03
95% earns
the 5%

This is the one people misread. It's not an argument to slack on the fundamentals and spend all your energy on the wow moment. It's the opposite. You can only do something extraordinary if the ordinary is already perfect. The magic lives in the last 5% — but you can only get there by nailing the first 95.

Guidara was ruthless about the foundations. Food had to be exceptional. Service had to be seamless. Not because those things were the point, but because sloppiness in the fundamentals burns the trust you need to take the leap.

Once the foundation is solid, the 5% becomes the creative space. The part where you stop executing and start expressing something.

At Big Spaceship

Our craft, our process, our communication — that's the 95%. When those are tight, we earn the room to do something unexpected. When they're not, we spend all our energy on the basics and never get to the part that's actually memorable.

04
Hospitality is
a team sport

One of Guidara's most important moves was distributing ownership of the guest experience across every person in the restaurant. Not just the maître d'. Everyone. A busser who overheard that a guest had never tried a New York hot dog could grab one from a street cart and bring it to the table. That person had the authority — and the encouragement — to act on what they noticed.

This only works when the culture is right. When people feel trusted enough to make the call without asking permission. When they know that a failed attempt at something extraordinary is celebrated, not penalized. When everyone understands the intent — to make people feel seen — well enough to improvise toward it.

Hospitality at scale isn't about one person doing something remarkable. It's about a team where remarkable is everyone's job.

At Big Spaceship

The best client moments don't always come from the most senior person in the room. They come from whoever was paying closest attention. Do we have a culture where anyone on the team feels empowered to make that call?

Questions worth sitting with

Carry this
into the room.

  • —Where in our work do we stop at 95% — and what would it look like to go further?
  • —Who is the person on the other end of what we're making, and what would make them feel genuinely seen?
  • —What's the unreasonable version of this? The one no one asked for but everyone remembers?
  • —What small thing, done differently, becomes the only thing they tell people about?
Now go make something

Don't be
reasonable.

The brief isn't to do something good. It's to do something that makes someone feel something. You already know how to be good at this job. Today, go be unreasonable about it.
